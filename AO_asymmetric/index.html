Guidestar-free adaptive optics with asymmetric apertures. Our system combines asymmetric apertures with machine learning to image through severe optical aberrations without wavefront sensors or guidestars. By contrast, conventional symmetric apertures suffer from a conjugate-flip ambiguity, which makes computational wavefront sensing and adaptive optics ineffective.

Abstract

This work introduces the first closed-loop adaptive optics (AO) system capable of optically correcting aberrations in real-time without a guidestar or a wavefront sensor. Nearly 40 years ago, it was demonstrated that asymmetric apertures enable phase retrieval algorithms to perform fully computational wavefront sensing. Inspired by this, we introduce a guidestar-free AO framework built around asymmetric apertures and machine learning. Our approach combines an asymmetric aperture placed in the optical path, a pair of machine learning algorithms that estimate the PSF from natural scene measurements, and a spatial light modulator that performs optical correction. Our method outperforms state-of-the-art guidestar-free wavefront shaping methods, using an order of magnitude fewer measurements and three orders of magnitude less computation.

Breaking Symmetry with Asymmetric Apertures

For a circular aperture, the original phase and its conjugate-flipped counterpart produce identical PSFs, leading to ambiguity. In contrast, a triangular aperture yields distinct PSFs, enabling unique phase retrieval and resolving the ambiguity.

System Overview

Overview of our proposed closed-loop guidestar-free AO system. Light passes through an obscurant and an asymmetric aperture before reaching an SLM. A PSF U-Net estimates the PSF from the captured image, and a Phase U-Net predicts the phase error to drive optical correction.
